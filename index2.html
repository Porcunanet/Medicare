Course 1 Key Terms
Medicare Part AHospital Insurance. Covers inpatient hospital care, skilled nursing facility care, home health care, and hospice care under Original Medicare.
Inpatient StatusFormally admitted to a hospital by a physician. Required for Part A coverage of a hospital stay. Distinct from observation/outpatient status.
Observation StatusAn outpatient classification for patients under hospital monitoring who have not been formally admitted. Covered under Part B, not Part A — and does not count toward SNF qualifying stay.
Benefit PeriodThe framework Medicare uses to measure inpatient hospital and SNF use. Begins on day of inpatient admission; ends after 60 consecutive days out of a hospital or SNF. Can have multiple per year.
3-Day Qualifying StayA requirement for Part A SNF coverage: the beneficiary must have been an inpatient in a hospital for at least 3 consecutive days before entering the SNF. Observation days do not count.
Skilled Nursing Facility (SNF)A facility providing post-hospital skilled nursing care, therapy, and other medical services. Part A covers up to 100 days per benefit period under qualifying conditions.
HomeboundA Medicare requirement for home health benefits — the beneficiary must be unable to leave home without considerable effort. Occasional absences for medical care or short trips are permitted.
Hospice CareNon-curative, palliative care for individuals with a terminal illness and a life expectancy of 6 months or less. The beneficiary elects hospice instead of curative care for the terminal condition.
Lifetime Reserve DaysAfter 90 inpatient days in a benefit period, Medicare provides 60 lifetime reserve days (days 91–150) that can only be used once in a lifetime. A daily coinsurance applies.
Part A PremiumMost beneficiaries pay $0 if they or a spouse worked 40+ quarters (10 years). Those with fewer quarters pay a monthly premium that varies by year. Disability recipients do not owe a Part A premium.

Course 2 Key Terms
Medicare Part BMedical Insurance. Covers medically necessary outpatient services, physician services, preventive care, home health care, DME, and ambulance services.
Outpatient CareCare received at a hospital, clinic, or doctor's office where the patient is not formally admitted as an inpatient. Part B covers most outpatient services when medically necessary.
Medically NecessaryServices or supplies that are needed to diagnose or treat a medical condition and meet accepted standards of medical practice. A requirement for Part B coverage.
Preventive CareScreenings, counseling, and services intended to prevent illness or detect conditions early. Most Part B preventive services are free with no deductible required.
Durable Medical Equipment (DME)Equipment that serves a medical purpose, can withstand repeated use, is appropriate for home use, and is expected to last 3+ years. Examples: wheelchairs, walkers, blood glucose monitors.
Part B PremiumA monthly fee everyone with Part B pays. The amount is set annually by CMS. It is usually deducted automatically from Social Security benefits. Higher-income beneficiaries pay an IRMAA surcharge.
Part B DeductibleAn annual amount the beneficiary must pay out-of-pocket before Part B begins covering services. Set by CMS each year. Applies to most Part B-covered services (not most preventive care).
20% CoinsuranceAfter the Part B deductible is met, the beneficiary pays 20% of the Medicare-approved amount for most covered services. Original Medicare pays the remaining 80%.
Participating ProviderA provider that has signed an agreement to accept Medicare's approved amount as full payment. Beneficiaries pay the least when seeing a participating provider.
Non-Participating ProviderA provider that accepts Medicare but has not signed the participating agreement. They can charge up to 15% above Medicare's approved amount (the "limiting charge"), resulting in higher costs for the beneficiary.
Opt-Out ProviderA provider who has completely opted out of the Medicare program. Medicare pays nothing for services from opt-out providers (except emergencies). The provider may charge any amount they choose.
AssignmentWhen a provider agrees to accept Medicare's approved amount as full payment for a service. Participating providers always take assignment; non-participating providers may take it case by case.

Part D provides prescription drug coverage available only through private insurance plans. Beneficiaries must have Part A and/or Part B to enroll. Coverage is offered as a stand-alone plan or bundled within Medicare Advantage.

Part D Costs
Part D costs include a monthly premium (possibly with a late enrollment penalty), an annual deductible set by CMS, and copays/coinsurance that vary by the plan's tier system. Using in-network pharmacies reduces costs.

Course 3 Key Terms
Medicare Part DPrescription drug coverage available only through private insurance plans. Beneficiaries must have Part A and/or Part B to enroll. Available as a stand-alone plan (PDP) or bundled in Medicare Advantage (MA-PD).
Creditable CoverageDrug coverage that is at least as good as standard Part D coverage. Beneficiaries with creditable coverage can delay Part D enrollment without incurring a late enrollment penalty.
FormularyThe list of prescription drugs covered by a Part D plan. Each plan has its own formulary. Formularies can change each year and mid-year with Medicare approval. Beneficiaries can formally request coverage of a non-formulary drug.
Price TierA cost category assigned to drugs on the formulary. Lower tiers (generic, preferred brand) cost less; higher tiers (non-preferred, specialty) cost more. Beneficiaries may request a tiering exception to lower their drug's cost.
Deductible PhaseThe first Part D coverage phase, during which the beneficiary pays the full cost of covered drugs until they meet the annual deductible (if the plan has one). CMS sets the maximum deductible each year.
Initial Coverage PeriodThe second Part D phase. The beneficiary and plan share the cost of covered drugs — the beneficiary pays a copay or coinsurance, and the plan pays the rest. Continues until the annual out-of-pocket spending cap (TrOOP) is reached.
Catastrophic CoverageThe third and final Part D coverage phase. Begins once true out-of-pocket (TrOOP) costs reach the annual cap. Beneficiary owes $0 for covered drugs for the rest of the calendar year.
TrOOP (True Out-of-Pocket Costs)The cumulative out-of-pocket costs that count toward the catastrophic coverage threshold. Includes the deductible, copays/coinsurance during the initial coverage period, and payments made by qualifying third parties on the beneficiary's behalf.
Prior AuthorizationA utilization management tool requiring plan approval before the drug is covered. The beneficiary's doctor typically provides evidence that the specific drug is medically necessary for the condition.
Step TherapyA coverage restriction requiring the beneficiary to first try a less expensive drug before the plan will cover the prescribed drug. If cheaper alternatives fail or are contraindicated, the plan should cover the original drug.
Quantity LimitsA utilization management tool that caps the amount of a drug the plan will cover in a given time period. Example: a plan may cover only a specific number of pills per month.
Transition FillA one-time, 30-day supply of a drug the beneficiary was already taking, provided when they switch to a new plan or when their existing plan changes coverage. Allows time to work with the doctor on alternative coverage or exception requests.

📅
Medigap Open Enrollment Period
Slide 15
Medigap Open Enrollment Period
A 6-month window beginning when the beneficiary is both 65+ and enrolled in Part B. During OEP, insurers must sell any plan at the best available rate and cannot deny coverage regardless of health status or pre-existing conditions.

Slide 17

Right to buy Medigap without medical underwriting within 63 days of losing certain coverage. Applies to beneficiaries 65+. Some states extend rights to those under 65. Triggers include: MA plan leaves service area, retiree coverage ends.

Course 4 Key Terms
Medigap (Medicare Supplement Insurance)A standardized, supplemental private insurance policy that pays part or all of the costs Original Medicare does not cover — such as deductibles, coinsurance, and copays. Does not work with Medicare Advantage.
Standardized Plans (A–N)The 10 lettered Medigap plan types (A, B, C, D, F, G, K, L, M, N). By law, a plan with the same letter must offer the same benefits regardless of which insurance company sells it. Premiums can vary by company.
Medicare SELECTA type of Medigap that requires beneficiaries to use a specific network of hospitals and doctors for non-emergency services to receive full benefits. Offers the same coverage as the equivalent lettered plan but at a lower premium.
Part B Excess ChargesThe additional amount (up to 15% above Medicare's approved amount) that non-participating providers can charge beneficiaries. Only Medigap Plans F and G cover excess charges.
Medigap Open Enrollment Period (OEP)A 6-month federally protected window beginning when a beneficiary is both age 65+ and enrolled in Medicare Part B. During OEP, insurers cannot deny coverage or charge higher premiums based on health status.
Guaranteed Issue RightThe federally protected right (for beneficiaries 65+) to purchase a Medigap without medical underwriting within 63 days of losing certain types of coverage. Insurers cannot deny coverage or charge extra based on health.
Pre-Existing Condition Waiting PeriodA period of up to 6 months imposed by insurers during which a Medigap will not cover services related to a pre-existing condition. Reduced month-for-month by prior creditable coverage.
Medical UnderwritingThe process insurers use to review a beneficiary's health history before deciding whether to sell a Medigap, and at what premium. Outside protected periods, insurers can use underwriting to deny coverage or charge higher premiums.
Community-Rated PricingA Medigap pricing method where the same premium is charged to all policyholders in the same geographic area, regardless of age. Premiums change only due to inflation or other non-age factors.
Issue Age-Rated PricingA Medigap pricing method where the premium is based on the beneficiary's age at the time of purchase. The premium does not increase due to age alone, though it may increase due to inflation.
Attained Age-Rated PricingA Medigap pricing method where the premium increases as the beneficiary gets older. Usually the least expensive initially but can become the most expensive over time.
30-Day Free Look PeriodA window during which a beneficiary who buys a new Medigap can keep their old policy while trying the new one, paying both premiums. After 30 days they must decide which to keep.

Medicare Advantage Basics
⭐
What is Medicare Advantage?
Slide 3
What is Medicare Advantage?
Established in 1999 as Medicare + Choice. Private plans contract with Medicare and are paid to provide benefits. Bundles Parts A, B, and usually D (Part C). Cannot be combined with a Medigap policy.
Overview →

Slide 4
MA Plan Enrollment
First-time enrollees use the Initial Coverage Election Period (ICEP). SEPs allow enrollment when moving out of a plan's service area. Around 50% of Medicare beneficiaries are now enrolled in MA Plans.
Enrollment →

Original Medicare vs. Medicare Advantage
Key differences: network requirements, vision/dental/hearing extras, referral rules, out-of-pocket limits, and Medigap eligibility. Case: Kimiko travels nationally — Original Medicare fits her lifestyle best.

Types of Medicare Advantage Plans
🏥
HMO Plans
Slide 8
Health Maintenance Organizations (HMOs)
Pay least for in-network services. Requires choosing a primary care physician and referral to see a specialist. May cover dental, vision, hearing. Cannot charge more than Original Medicare for chemotherapy, dialysis, or SNF care.
HMO →
🌍
PPO Plans
Slide 9
Preferred Provider Organizations (PPOs)
Covers both in-network (cheapest) and out-of-network care. No referral required for specialists. Regional PPOs offer expanded service area. Limit on out-of-pocket costs. Can charge more than Original Medicare for home health care.
PPO →
💲
PFFS Plans

Private Fee-for-Service (PFFS) Plans
Cannot restrict beneficiary to a network. Out-of-network providers choose to contract with the plan. No prior authorization or specialist referral required. Costs include additional premium plus deductible, copay, and coinsurance.
PFFS →
🎯
Special Needs Plans (SNPs)
Slides 11–12
Special Needs Plans (SNPs)
Designed for beneficiaries with specific conditions. Must include prescription drug coverage. Three types: C-SNPs (chronic conditions), I-SNPs (institutional/nursing home), D-SNPs (dual Medicare-Medicaid eligible).
SNP →
🏦
MSA Plans
Slides 13–15
Medicare Medical Savings Account (MSA) Plans
Two parts: high-deductible health plan + savings account. Plan deposits money into the MSA; beneficiary can use funds for health costs. No drug coverage or provider network. No additional premium beyond Part B.
MSA →

Additional MA Benefits
Slide 21
Additional MA Benefits
MA Plans can offer coverage beyond Original Medicare: extra SNF days past day 100, dental, vision, and hearing services. SNPs may have more flexibility for non-medical benefits (e.g., a scale for someone with chronic heart failure).
Extra Benefits →
⚠️
Premium Non-Payment
Slide 20
Premium Non-Payment & Disenrollment
MA Plans can disenroll members who fail to pay premiums. Plan must send bill and written non-payment notice with at least a 2-month grace period. Disenrolled beneficiaries are automatically returned to Original Medicare.
Disenrollment →
🔒
Beneficiary Protections
Slides 22–23
Beneficiary Protections
MA Plans cannot deny enrollment based on health history, disability, or genetics; cannot discriminate or inhibit access. Emergency services must be covered even out-of-network with no prior authorization. SEP available if CMS terminates the plan.
Protections →
🌟
Star Ratings
Slides 24–25
CMS Star Ratings
CMS rates all MA and stand-alone Part D plans 1–5 stars across 5 categories. Plans with 4–5 stars earn quality bonus payments. Plans with 5 stars can enroll beneficiaries year-round. Available in Medicare Plan Finder.
Star Ratings →
📝
Grievances
Slide 26
Filing a Grievance
A grievance is a complaint about inadequate services (not an appeal). File with Medicare (call 1-800-MEDICARE or use online form) or directly with the plan within 60 days. Plan must respond within 60 days, or 24 hours for expedited grievances.
Grievances →
Course 5 Key Terms
Medicare Advantage (Part C)A private plan that contracts with Medicare to provide all Part A and B benefits (and usually Part D drug coverage) in one plan. Established in 1999 as Medicare + Choice. Cannot be combined with a Medigap policy.
Health Maintenance Organization (HMO)A type of MA Plan where beneficiaries pay the least for in-network services, must choose a primary care physician, and need a referral to see a specialist. May offer dental, vision, and hearing coverage.
Preferred Provider Organization (PPO)A type of MA Plan that covers both in-network (cheapest) and out-of-network care. No referral required for specialists. Regional PPOs offer expanded service areas.
Private Fee-for-Service (PFFS)A type of MA Plan that cannot restrict beneficiaries to a network. Out-of-network providers choose whether to contract. No prior authorization or specialist referral required.
Special Needs Plan (SNP)MA Plans designed for beneficiaries with specific needs. Must include drug coverage. Types: C-SNPs (chronic conditions like cancer or diabetes), I-SNPs (institutional/nursing home residents), and D-SNPs (dual Medicare-Medicaid eligible).
Medical Savings Account (MSA) PlanAn MA Plan with two parts: a high-deductible health plan and a savings account the plan funds. Beneficiary uses MSA or own funds until deductible is met. No drug coverage or provider network; no additional premium beyond Part B.
Initial Coverage Election Period (ICEP)The MA plan enrollment period for beneficiaries who first sign up for Parts A and B during their Initial Enrollment Period (IEP). It aligns with the IEP and allows the beneficiary to elect an MA Plan for the first time.
Special Enrollment Period (SEP)A time outside regular enrollment periods when a Medicare beneficiary can enroll in, switch, or disenroll from an MA Plan due to a qualifying event — such as moving out of the plan's service area.
Out-of-Pocket MaximumThe annual limit on how much a beneficiary enrolled in an MA Plan must pay out-of-pocket for covered services. Original Medicare has no such limit. MA Plans are required to have one, providing financial protection.
CMS Star RatingA 1–5 star quality rating CMS assigns to all MA and stand-alone Part D plans based on performance across 5 categories. Plans with 4–5 stars earn quality bonuses; 5-star plans can enroll members year-round.
GrievanceA complaint a beneficiary makes to Medicare or their MA plan when they feel the plan is not providing adequate services. A grievance is different from an appeal. Filed within 60 days of the event; plan must respond within 60 days (24 hours for expedited).
Complaint Tracking Module (CTM)Medicare's system for tracking grievances filed by beneficiaries. Accessed by calling 1-800-MEDICARE or submitting an online form. CMS uses CTM data for quality assurance and plan oversight.
