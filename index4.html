Medicare and Employer Insurance

35 minutes seat time 4 learning objectives GHP, COBRA, Retiree & FEHB
Learning Objectives
1

Explain coordination of benefits and differentiate between primary and secondary insurance.

2

Determine when a beneficiary's current employee insurance pays primary or secondary to Medicare.

3

Understand how COBRA, retiree insurance, and FEHB coordinate with Medicare.

4

Advise beneficiaries about when they may or may not want to delay Medicare enrollment.

Coordination of Benefits
🤝
Coordination of Benefits Overview
Slide 3
Coordination of Benefits
Rules that determine how Medicare and other insurance share responsibility for paying claims. Primary payer pays first; secondary pays second. Managed by the BCRC (855-798-2627).
Overview→
👔
Current Employee: Age 65+
Slide 4
Current Employee Health Insurance: 65+
Medicare is primary for small GHPs (<20 employees). Medicare is secondary when GHP has 20+ employees. Applies when beneficiary is enrolled in Medicare and covered by their or their spouse's current employer plan.
Age 65+→
🛡️
Protections: GHP 20+ Employees
Slide 5
Protections for GHP Members (20+ Employees)
GHPs of 20+ employees must provide the same coverage as for all other employees and cannot discriminate against Medicare-eligible individuals. Employers cannot encourage employees to drop the employer plan.
Protections→
🦽
Current Employee: Under 65
Slide 6
Current Employee Health Insurance: Under 65
For beneficiaries under 65 eligible due to disability. Medicare is primary if GHP has fewer than 100 employees. Medicare is secondary if GHP is a large group health plan (LGHP) of 100+ employees.
Under 65→
💳
Medicare as Secondary Payer
Slide 7
Medicare as Secondary Payer
When GHP (20+) or LGHP pays primary, Medicare pays secondary. If GHP denies a Medicare-covered service, Medicare may pay primary. GHP denial does not affect Medicare's own coverage decisions.
Secondary Payer→

Delaying Medicare Enrollment
⏳
Delaying Part A & B Enrollment
Slide 8
Delaying Part A and/or Part B
Two questions to consider: (1) Will other insurance pay primary? If secondary, delaying is usually unadvisable. (2) Will there be delays, gaps, or penalties? Current employee coverage provides a Part B SEP.
Delay Risks→
💊
Delaying Part D Enrollment
Slide 9
Delaying Part D Enrollment
Individuals covered by employer drug coverage have a Part D SEP. No LEP if covered by creditable drug coverage. Keep all health insurance records. Delaying without creditable coverage = likely Part D LEP.
Part D→
COBRA, Retiree Insurance, ESRD & FEHB
🔗
COBRA Continuation Coverage
Slides 10–13
COBRA Continuation Coverage
Federal law giving individuals right to continued coverage after job loss, divorce, death, or other qualifying events. If enrolled in COBRA after Medicare, Medicare pays primary. Enroll in Medicare when first eligible — COBRA provides no Part B SEP.
COBRA→
🎓
Retiree Insurance
Slide 14
Retiree Insurance
Coverage for former employees no longer actively working. Medicare always pays primary; retiree insurance is secondary. Delaying Medicare while on retiree coverage = no Part B SEP later. Drug coverage SEP available 63 days after losing retiree coverage.
Retiree→
🏥
End-Stage Renal Disease
Slide 15
End-Stage Renal Disease (ESRD)
Special 30-month coordination period applies when both Medicare (ESRD) and a GHP are active. GHP pays primary during the coordination period. Medicare pays primary after 30 months. GHP can be from current or former employer.
ESRD→
🏛️
Federal Employee Health Benefits
Slides 18–19
Federal Employee Health Benefits (FEHB)
Covers current and retired federal employees; administered by OPM. Currently working: FEHB pays primary. Retired: Medicare pays primary. Disenrolling from FEHB is permanent — cannot re-enroll. Retirees who don't take Part B lose their Part B SEP.
FEHB→

Enrollment Decision Considerations
📅
Right to Part B SEP
Slide 20
Right to Part B Special Enrollment Period
Individuals with current employee insurance have a Part B SEP to enroll when coverage ends — regardless of employer size. COBRA and retiree coverage do not count as current employee insurance and do not provide a Part B SEP.
SEP→
⚠️
Payment & Coverage Issues
Slide 20
Possibility of Payment or Coverage Issues
If other insurance pays secondary to Medicare, delaying enrollment is usually unadvisable. GHP may not pay on claims where Medicare hasn't paid first, and may recoup any payments made as primary.
Risk→
💊
Medicare Part D
Slide 20
Medicare Part D Consideration
Employer drug coverage may be creditable — preventing a Part D LEP. Verify creditability annually and keep records of all coverage periods. SEP available up to 2 months after losing employer drug coverage.
Part D→
🏛️
FEHB Considerations
Slide 20
Federal Employee Health Benefits
Retiree FEHB beneficiaries who don't take Part B when first eligible cannot get a Part B SEP later. Disenrolling from FEHB is permanent. Two options: enroll in Part B and pay both premiums, or delay and risk a Part B LEP.
FEHB→
Key Terms
Coordination of Benefits
Rules determining how Medicare and other insurance share responsibility for paying claims, including which insurer pays first (primary) and which pays second (secondary).
Primary Insurance
The insurance that pays first on health care claims, before any other coverage pays its portion.
Secondary Insurance
The insurance that pays after the primary payer has paid its share, potentially covering remaining costs.
Group Health Plan (GHP)
Health insurance provided to actively working individuals through their employer or union. Coordination rules depend on whether the employer has fewer or more than 20 employees (for age 65+).
BCRC (Benefits Coordination & Recovery Center)
Medicare contractor that ensures claims are paid in the correct order. Contact: 855-798-2627.
COBRA Continuation Coverage
Federal law giving individuals the right to purchase continued employer health coverage after job loss, divorce, death, or other qualifying events. Does not provide a Part B SEP.
Retiree Insurance
Coverage for former employees no longer actively working. Medicare always pays primary; retiree insurance is secondary. Does not provide a Part B SEP if Medicare enrollment is delayed.
FEHB (Federal Employee Health Benefits)
Program for current and retired federal employees administered by OPM (www.opm.gov). Federal government is a large employer. Disenrolling from FEHB is permanent.
LGHP (Large Group Health Plan)
A group health plan from an employer with 100+ employees. Pays primary to Medicare for disability-based beneficiaries under age 65.
ESRD (End-Stage Renal Disease)
Permanent kidney failure requiring dialysis or transplant. Special 30-month coordination period applies — GHP pays primary during this time; Medicare pays primary after.
Part B Special Enrollment Period (SEP)
Window to enroll in Part B without penalty when current employee coverage ends, regardless of employer size. COBRA and retiree coverage do not qualify.
Creditable Drug Coverage
Prescription drug coverage at least as good as Part D. Having creditable employer drug coverage allows Part D delay without a late enrollment penalty.

Military Benefits: TRICARE & VA
🎖️
TRICARE Overview
Slide 3
Military Benefits: TRICARE
TRICARE covers active duty service members (ADSMs) and their families (ADFMs). TRICARE pays primary and Medicare pays secondary. TRICARE for Life (TFL) covers those retired from military who also have Medicare — Medicare pays primary, TFL pays secondary.
TRICARE→
📋
TFL and Medicare Enrollment
Slide 4
TFL and Medicare Enrollment
Individual 65+ covered by TRICARE through active duty can delay Part B and remain eligible. They may use Part B SEP when TRICARE ends. Those NOT covered through active duty should sign up for Medicare to avoid losing TRICARE coverage.
Enrollment→
🦅
Veterans Affairs Benefits
Slide 5
Veterans Affairs (VA) Benefits
VA benefits cover individuals with disability related to active duty service. VA does not coordinate with Medicare at all. VA covers some services Medicare doesn't (e.g., custodial care). VA drug coverage is creditable. Enroll in Medicare when first eligible — no Part B SEP available.
VA→

Liability, No-Fault Insurance & Workers' Compensation
⚖️
Liability, No-Fault & Workers' Comp
Slide 8
Liability, No-Fault & Workers' Compensation
Liability insurance protects policyholders if legally liable for injury. No-fault insurance pays for care from accidents regardless of fault. Workers' compensation pays for job-related injury claims. Medicare pays secondary to all three types.
Secondary Payer→
🏛️
BCRC & Conditional Payments
Slide 9
BCRC and Conditional Payments
Medicare can make conditional payments as primary payer. The BCRC manages a 6-step recovery process — from reporting the case, through issuing payment summaries, to issuing a recovery demand letter after settlement is received.
BCRC Process→
Long-Term Care & Dental Insurance
🏠
Long-Term Care Insurance
Slide 10
Long-Term Care Insurance
Covers long-term services and support (LTSS), also called custodial care — help with activities of daily living (ADLs). Medicare does not cover custodial care alone. Long-term care policies fill the gap. Purchase before you need it.
Long-Term Care→
✅
LTC Qualifications
Slide 11
Long-Term Care Qualifications
Two triggers: (1) Benefit trigger — needing help with 2+ ADLs (bathing, dressing, eating, etc.) or cognitive impairment such as Alzheimer's. (2) Elimination period — 30-, 60-, or 90-day waiting period before the policy starts paying.
Qualifications→
💰
LTC Policy Costs
Slide 12
Long-Term Care Costs
LTC policy costs depend on: age at purchase, maximum number of days the policy will pay (maximum policy benefits), maximum daily benefit amount, and any optional benefits. Long-Term Care Partnership Programs may protect Medicaid assets.
Costs→
🦷
Dental Insurance
Slide 13
Dental Insurance
Most dental care (cleanings, fillings, extractions, dentures) is excluded from Medicare. Some Medicare Advantage plans offer limited dental coverage. Standalone dental policies can be purchased privately. In rare overlap cases, Medicare is primary and dental plan is secondary.
Dental→
Health Insurance Marketplaces
🏪
Health Insurance Marketplaces
Slide 14
Health Insurance Marketplaces
Uninsured individuals can purchase Marketplace coverage. Medicare beneficiaries generally do not need to use Marketplaces — Medigap, MA plans, and Part D plans are not sold through the Marketplace. Tax credits are lost once enrolled in Medicare.
Marketplace→
Key Terms
TRICARE
Military health insurance for active duty service members (ADSMs) and active duty family members (ADFMs). TRICARE pays primary; Medicare pays secondary when a beneficiary has both.
TRICARE for Life (TFL)
TRICARE coverage for individuals who are retired from the military and have Medicare. Requires enrollment in both Part A and Part B. Medicare pays primary; TFL pays secondary. TFL drug coverage is generally creditable.
VA Benefits
Coverage for individuals with a disability related to active duty military service. VA benefits do not coordinate with Medicare at all. VA covers some services Medicare doesn't, such as custodial and nursing home care. VA drug coverage is creditable.
Liability Insurance
Insurance that protects the policyholder if they are legally liable for someone else's injury. Medicare pays secondary to liability insurance. Medicare can make conditional payments and recover through the BCRC.
No-Fault Insurance
Insurance that pays for health care services resulting from an individual's injury as the result of an accident, regardless of who was at fault. Medicare pays secondary to no-fault insurance.
Workers' Compensation
A state-administered program that pays for health care claims for job-related injuries. Medicare pays secondary to workers' compensation. The BCRC manages conditional payment and recovery processes.
Long-Term Care (LTC) Insurance
Private insurance that covers long-term services and support (LTSS), also called custodial care — assistance with activities of daily living. Medicare does not cover custodial care alone. LTC policies fill this gap.
Benefit Trigger
The criteria that must be met before a long-term care policy begins paying. Typically requires the individual to need help with 2 or more activities of daily living (ADLs), or to have a cognitive impairment such as Alzheimer's disease.
Elimination Period
The amount of time that must pass before a long-term care policy starts covering the beneficiary's care. Commonly 30, 60, or 90 days. Similar to a deductible measured in time rather than money.
ADLs (Activities of Daily Living)
Basic self-care tasks used to assess eligibility for long-term care benefits: bathing, dressing, using the toilet, transferring (moving from bed to chair), caring for incontinence, and eating.
Health Insurance Marketplace
A resource where uninsured and some underinsured individuals can purchase coverage. Medicare beneficiaries generally do not need to use Marketplaces, and enrolling in Medicare causes loss of Marketplace tax credits.
SHOP (Small Business Health Options Program)
A Marketplace program for small businesses. SHOP plans coordinate with Medicare the same way other current employee insurance does, following the same group health plan rules.

What is Medicaid?
🏛️
Medicaid Overview
Slide 3
What is Medicaid?
Federal and state program for people with limited income and assets. Federal guidelines set the framework; states determine income/asset limits, service scope, provider payment, and program names.
Overview→
📋
Medicaid Options
Slide 4
Medicaid Options
Three main program types: Medicaid for residents of institution (nursing homes/SNFs), Medicaid waiver programs (home/community care), and Medicaid for residents of community (general health services).
Programs→
👓
ABD Medicaid
Slide 5
Medicaid Eligibility: Aged, Blind, Disabled (ABD)
Traditional Medicaid — most Medicare beneficiaries with Medicaid have ABD Medicaid. Income must be below 74% FPL in most states. SSI recipients automatically qualify. States use SSI budgeting methods.
ABD→
Meeting Income Limits
💸
Medicaid Spend-Down
Slides 6–7
Medicaid Spend-Down
Some states offer the Medically Needy Program — individuals deduct medical expenses from income to meet the ABD spend-down limit. Spend-down is the difference between income and limit over a set period.
Spend-Down→
🔒
Trusts
Slide 7
Trusts
Available in some states as an alternative to spend-down. Special Needs Trusts, Supplemental Needs Trusts, and Miller Trusts keep income/assets from counting for Medicaid purposes. Strict rules apply.
Trusts→
💼
Medicaid Buy-In
Slide 8
Medicaid Buy-In Program
Available in some states for individuals under 65 with a disability who want to work and still receive Medicaid benefits. Qualifying individuals pay a premium to the Medicaid program.
Buy-In→
📊
Spend-Down Example
Slide 9
Medicaid Spend-Down Example: Vivian
Vivian's income is $100 above her state's limit. She can use $100 in medical bills to qualify for Medicaid through spend-down — but only in months where she accumulates enough qualifying medical expenses.
Example→
Application Process
📝
ABD Medicaid Application
Slide 10
Application Process for ABD Medicaid
Four steps: (1) Apply through local Medicaid office (online, mail, in-person, or via community org), (2) Submit required documents, (3) Request fair hearing if denied, (4) Recertify annually.
Application→
Medicaid Benefits
✅
Mandatory Benefits
Slide 11
Mandatory Medicaid Benefits
All state Medicaid programs must cover: inpatient/outpatient hospital services, nursing facility services, home health services, physician/lab/x-ray services, transportation to medical services, and more.
Mandatory→

Optional Benefits
Slide 12
Optional Medicaid Benefits
States may offer: prescription drugs, dental services and dentures, optometry and eyeglasses, physical/occupational/speech therapy, chiropractic services, hospice care, personal care, podiatry, and more.
Optional→

🤝
Dual Eligible
Slide 13
Medicare and Medicaid Coordination
Dual-eligible individuals are enrolled in both Medicare and Medicaid. Medicare always pays first; Medicaid is payer of last resort. Full dual-eligible individuals typically have no Part A/B premiums, deductibles, or copays.
Dual-Eligible→

Medicaid for Institution Residents
Slide 16
Medicaid for Residents of Institution
All states have this program. Covers nursing facility services and general health care. Individual must need nursing home level of care and meet income/asset guidelines. Medicaid can supplement Medicare-covered skilled nursing care.
Institution→
⚠️
Institution Considerations
Slide 17
Medicaid for Residents of Institution: Considerations
Key considerations: income and assets of both spouses are reviewed together; five-year look-back period for asset transfers; home ownership affects Medicaid eligibility — consult an elder law attorney.
Considerations→
🏠
HCBS Waiver Programs
Slide 18
Medicaid HCBS Waiver Programs
Home and Community-Based Services (HCBS) waiver programs help beneficiaries stay at home or in community settings. Covered services may include personal care, skilled nursing, therapy, and home modifications.
Waiver→
📌
Waiver Program Considerations
Slide 19
Medicaid Waiver Program Considerations
Key considerations: income/assets of individual and spouse reviewed together; states generally have limits on the number of people who can receive waiver benefits (wait lists may apply); home ownership affects eligibility.
Considerations→
MAGI Medicaid & Transitions
📈
MAGI Medicaid
Slide 20
MAGI Medicaid Basics
Modified Adjusted Gross Income (MAGI) determines eligibility for community Medicaid for children, pregnant people, parents/caregivers, and childless adults under 65. Available in states that expanded Medicaid under the ACA.
MAGI→
📋
MAGI Eligibility
Slide 21
MAGI Medicaid Eligibility
Available to eligible individuals with income below 138% FPL. Resources and assets are not counted. Certain income excluded (workers' comp, child support, veterans' benefits). Enroll through state or federal Health Insurance Marketplace.
Eligibility→
🔄
MAGI to Medicare Transitions
Slide 22
MAGI Medicaid to Medicare Transitions
When a MAGI Medicaid individual becomes Medicare-eligible, they should be evaluated for ABD Medicaid. MAGI has more generous income limits than ABD. Five key steps guide the transition process.
Transitions→
🎯
Transition Outcomes
Slide 23
Possible Transition Outcomes
Three possible outcomes when transitioning from MAGI Medicaid to Medicare: (1) with ABD Medicaid + MSP, (2) with MSP only, or (3) without Medicaid or MSP — with alternative coverage options.

Key Terms
Medicaid
A joint federal and state program that provides health coverage to people with limited income and assets. States administer Medicaid within federal guidelines, determining income/asset limits, services, and provider payment rates.
ABD Medicaid (Aged, Blind, Disabled)
Traditional Medicaid for aged, blind, or disabled individuals. Most Medicare beneficiaries who have Medicaid have ABD Medicaid. Income limit is generally below 74% FPL; SSI recipients automatically qualify in most states.
Dual-Eligible
An individual enrolled in both Medicare and Medicaid. Medicare always pays primary; Medicaid is the payer of last resort. Full dual-eligible individuals typically have no Part A/B premiums, deductibles, coinsurance, or copays.
Medicaid Spend-Down
A mechanism in some states (Medically Needy Program) allowing individuals to deduct medical expenses from income to meet the ABD Medicaid income limit. The spend-down amount is the difference between the individual's income and the state's limit.
MAGI Medicaid
Modified Adjusted Gross Income Medicaid — available in states that expanded Medicaid under the ACA. Covers certain individuals with income below 138% FPL; assets are not counted. Enrolled through the state or federal Health Insurance Marketplace.
HCBS Waiver Program
Home and Community-Based Services waiver programs that allow states to provide long-term care services in home or community settings rather than nursing facilities. Each state has at least one HCBS waiver program.
Five-Year Look-Back Period
A rule for institutional Medicaid that examines asset transfers made within the past five years. If assets were transferred in violation of Medicaid rules, the beneficiary may be penalized and temporarily ineligible for Medicaid coverage.
Miller Trust (Qualified Income Trust)
A type of trust used in some states to help individuals with income above the Medicaid limit qualify for benefits. Excess income is deposited into the trust, keeping it from counting toward Medicaid eligibility.
Medicaid Buy-In Program
A program available in some states allowing individuals under 65 with a disability to work and still receive Medicaid benefits by paying a premium to the Medicaid program.
SSI (Supplemental Security Income)
A federal income supplement program for aged, blind, and disabled individuals with limited income and resources. In most states, individuals receiving SSI automatically qualify for Medicaid.
Countable Income
The amount of income left over after certain types of earned and unearned income are deducted from an individual's monthly income for Medicaid eligibility purposes. Certain income and assets are excluded from this calculation.
MSP (Medicare Savings Program)
A Medicaid program that helps pay Medicare costs such as premiums, deductibles, and copays. Partial dual-eligible individuals have MSP. When a MAGI Medicaid beneficiary transitions to Medicare, they should be evaluated for ABD Medicaid and/or MSP.

Medicare Savings Programs (MSPs)
💰
MSP Overview
Slide 3
Medicare Savings Programs (MSPs)
State-administered benefits that help pay out-of-pocket Medicare costs for people with limited income/assets. Four types: QI, SLMB, QMB, and QDWI — each with different income limits and benefits.
Overview→
📊
MSP Benefits Table
Slide 4
MSP Types & Benefits
QI (135% FPL): pays Part B premium + 3-month retroactive reimbursement. SLMB (120% FPL): same. QMB (100% FPL): pays Part B + Part A premium + improper billing protection. QDWI (200% FPL): pays Part A premium only.
Benefits→
🚫
QMB Improper Billing
Slide 5
QMB Improper Billing Protections
Federal law prohibits providers from billing QMB beneficiaries for cost-sharing. Applies to Original Medicare and in-network MA plan providers. Protections apply even across state lines. Providers can be penalized for violations.
QMB Protection→
📋
Qualifying: Income
Slide 6
Qualifying for MSP: Income
Individual qualifies if income and assets fall below state limits. Federal gov sets minimums; states can be more generous. Key exclusions: first $20 of all income, first $65 of wages, half of remaining wages, food stamps.
Income→
🏠
Qualifying: Assets
Slide 7
Qualifying for MSP: Assets
Assets include savings, stocks, bonds, retirement accounts, real estate. Always excluded: primary home, one car, household goods/wedding rings, burial spaces up to $1,500/person, life insurance <$1,500 cash value.
Assets→
📝
MSP Application
Slide 8
MSP Application Process
5 steps: (1) Submit to local Medicaid office, (2) Gather documentation, (3) If approved, verify benefits begin on date listed, (4) Request fair hearing if denied, (5) Recertify annually. Benefits may take 3–4 months — reimbursement applies retroactively.
Application→
✅
Additional MSP Benefits
Slide 9
Additional MSP Benefits
MSP approval also: enrolls beneficiary in Part B outside usual enrollment periods (if Part A enrolled), enables QMB to get premium Part A outside usual periods, eliminates Part B LEP, and auto-enrolls individual in Extra Help through "deeming."
Benefits→
Extra Help (Low-Income Subsidy)
💊
Extra Help Overview
Slide 10
Extra Help (Low-Income Subsidy)
Also called Low-Income Subsidy (LIS). Federal program administered by SSA that helps pay Part D premiums, lower coinsurance/copays for covered medications. Works with stand-alone Part D or MA plans with drug coverage.
Extra Help→
🎁
Extra Help Benefits
Slide 11
Extra Help Benefits
Enrolls beneficiary in Part D if not already enrolled; eliminates Part D late enrollment penalty; allows beneficiary to change stand-alone Part D plans once per month (instead of only during AEP).
Benefits→
💵
Extra Help Costs
Slide 12
Extra Help Costs
With Extra Help: $0 premium if enrolled in a benchmark plan, no deductible, and low or no copays for covered drugs. Significantly reduces the cost burden of prescription medications for qualifying beneficiaries.
Costs→
🖊️
How to Apply
Slide 13
Extra Help Application Process
Apply via: SSA at 800-772-1213, local Social Security office, or online at ssa.gov. Also auto-enrolled via MSP, Medicaid, or SSI. If approved, beneficiary gets letter about benchmark plans. If denied, they can appeal.
Application→
🔄
LI NET Program
Slide 14
LI NET — Low-Income Newly Eligible Transition
Temporary program for Extra Help beneficiaries whose Part D plan coverage hasn't started yet. Eliminates coverage gaps. Example: Luis has Extra Help from March 5 but Part D starts April 1 — LI NET covers his prescriptions in between. Call 800-783-1307.
LI NET→
State & Manufacturer Drug Assistance Programs
🗺️
SPAPs
Slide 15
State Pharmaceutical Assistance Programs (SPAPs)
State-based programs that may help pay the cost of beneficiary's drugs. Not all states have SPAPs. Each may have its own eligibility rules, application instructions, and conditions. Contact State Dept. of Health or visit medicare.gov for info.
SPAPs→
🏭
PAPs
Slide 16
Pharmaceutical Assistance Programs (PAPs)
Manufacturer-funded programs offering discounts on brand-name or generic drugs. Some may not work if beneficiary has prescription drug coverage. Each program has its own eligibility requirements. Visit rxassist.org or needymeds.org for info.
PAPs→

Medicare Savings Programs (MSPs)
State Medicaid programs that help people with limited income and assets pay out-of-pocket Medicare costs such as premiums, deductibles, and coinsurance. Four types: QMB, SLMB, QI, and QDWI.
Qualified Medicare Beneficiary (QMB)
MSP for individuals with income at or below 100% FPL. Pays Part B premium (and Part A premium if applicable). Provides improper billing protection — providers cannot charge QMB beneficiaries for Medicare cost-sharing.
Specified Low-Income Medicare Beneficiary (SLMB)
MSP for individuals with income between 100–120% FPL. Pays the Part B premium and provides 3-month retroactive Part B premium reimbursement.
Qualifying Individual (QI)
MSP for individuals with income between 120–135% FPL. Pays the Part B premium and provides 3-month retroactive reimbursement. Funded by limited federal allotment — first-come, first-served annually.
Qualified Disabled and Working Individual (QDWI)
MSP for Medicare beneficiaries under 65 who are working, have a disability, and are not Medicaid-eligible. Income limit is 200% FPL. Pays the Part A premium only.
Extra Help (Low-Income Subsidy / LIS)
Federal program administered by the Social Security Administration that helps pay Medicare Part D premiums and reduces coinsurance/copays for covered medications. Beneficiaries approved for MSP, Medicaid, or SSI are automatically deemed eligible through a process called deeming.
Deeming
The automatic process by which individuals approved for MSP, Medicaid, or SSI are enrolled in Extra Help without having to apply separately. Simplifies access to the Low-Income Subsidy for eligible beneficiaries.
Benchmark Plan
A Medicare Part D plan available at no premium cost to Extra Help recipients. Beneficiaries not already enrolled in Part D are automatically enrolled in a benchmark plan when they receive Extra Help approval.
LI NET (Low-Income Newly Eligible Transition)
A temporary program that provides prescription drug coverage to Extra Help-eligible beneficiaries who are between plans or whose Part D plan coverage has not yet started. Eliminates coverage gaps. Contact: 800-783-1307.
State Pharmaceutical Assistance Programs (SPAPs)
State-run programs that may help pay the cost of prescription drugs for qualifying residents. Not available in all states. Each program has its own eligibility requirements, application process, and rules. Contact your State Department of Health for details.
Pharmaceutical Assistance Programs (PAPs)
Programs run by drug manufacturers that offer discounts or free medications for qualifying individuals who cannot afford their prescriptions. Some programs may not work if the beneficiary has prescription drug coverage. Visit rxassist.org or needymeds.org.
Best Available Evidence (BAE)
Documentation a beneficiary can show their pharmacist as proof of Extra Help eligibility while waiting for their Part D plan to begin. Includes the auto-enrollment Extra Help notice or a current Medicaid card. Used with the LI NET program.
